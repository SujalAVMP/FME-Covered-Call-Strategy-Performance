\begin{abstract}
A covered call strategy involves holding a long position in a stock while simultaneously selling a call option on the same stock. This report investigates the performance of this strategy using historical data for Reliance Industries (RELIANCE.NS), Monte Carlo simulations, and sensitivity analysis. We compare the covered call with a simple buy-and-hold approach across multiple risk-adjusted metrics including the Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, and Value-at-Risk. In our marked-to-market backtest with approximately monthly 21-trading-day rolls, the covered call lowers annualized volatility from 28.27\% to 24.83\% and improves maximum drawdown from -44.08\% to -40.56\%, while reducing annualized return from 15.39\% to 11.54\% because upside beyond the strike is surrendered.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Options are derivative instruments that grant the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a predetermined strike price before or at expiry. Among the various strategies that combine options with the underlying, the \textbf{covered call} is one of the most widely used.

A covered call consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a \textbf{long position} in the underlying asset (e.g., a stock), and
    \item a \textbf{short position} in a call option on the same asset.
\end{itemize}

By writing (selling) the call, the investor collects a premium upfront. In exchange, they give up any gains beyond the strike price $K$. This strategy is employed by investors who are \textbf{mildly bullish or neutral} on the underlying — they expect the stock to stay roughly where it is or rise modestly, and wish to generate additional income from the option premium.

The goal of this project is to:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Simulate the returns of a covered call strategy on a real stock.
    \item Compare the results with simply holding the stock (buy-and-hold).
    \item Analyze the risk reduction and capped upside inherent in the strategy.
\end{enumerate}

We implement the analysis in Python, using Black-Scholes pricing for option premiums, Geometric Brownian Motion for Monte Carlo simulations, and historical backtesting with data from Reliance Industries (NSE).

\section{Theoretical Framework}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Covered Call Payoff}

Let:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $S_0$ = current stock price,
    \item $S_T$ = stock price at expiry,
    \item $K$ = strike price of the call,
    \item $C_0$ = premium received from selling the call.
\end{itemize}

The payoff at expiry from the long stock position is $S_T$. The payoff from the short call is $-\max(S_T - K, 0)$. Combining:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{\text{covered call}}(S_T) = S_T - \max(S_T - K, 0) = \min(S_T, K)
\end{equation}

The \textbf{profit}, accounting for the initial cost and premium received:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Profit}(S_T) = \min(S_T, K) + C_0 - S_0
\end{equation}

Key properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Breakeven price}: $S_0 - C_0$ (lower than the stock-only breakeven of $S_0$).
    \item \textbf{Maximum profit}: $(K - S_0) + C_0$, achieved when $S_T \geq K$.
    \item \textbf{Maximum loss}: $S_0 - C_0$ (if the stock goes to zero; the premium provides a small cushion).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Black-Scholes Option Pricing}

We price the European call option using the Black-Scholes formula:
\begin{equation}
    C = S_0 \, N(d_1) - K \, e^{-rT} \, N(d_2)
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{align}
    d_1 &= \frac{\ln(S_0 / K) + (r + \sigma^2/2) \, T}{\sigma \sqrt{T}} \\[6pt]
    d_2 &= d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T}
\end{align}
$N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF, $r$ is the risk-free rate, $\sigma$ is the annualized volatility, and $T$ is time to expiry in years.

\subsection{Greeks of the Covered Call Position}

The Greeks quantify the sensitivity of the position to changes in market parameters. For the covered call (long stock + short call):

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{1.7cm} p{5.2cm} p{5.0cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Greek} & \textbf{Formula} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
    \midrule
    Delta & $1 - N(d_1)$ & Reduced directional exposure (between 0 and 1) \\
    Gamma & $-\dfrac{n(d_1)}{S \sigma \sqrt{T}}$ & Negative: position is hurt by large moves \\[8pt]
    Theta & $+\dfrac{S \, n(d_1) \, \sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} + r K e^{-rT} N(d_2)$ & Positive: time decay benefits the position \\[8pt]
    Vega & $-S \, n(d_1) \sqrt{T}$ & Negative: hurt by rising volatility \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\normalsize

Here $n(\cdot)$ denotes the standard normal PDF. The key takeaway is that the covered call has \textbf{positive theta} (profits from time decay) and \textbf{negative gamma} (harmed by large price swings in either direction).

\subsection{Risk Metrics}

We evaluate the strategies using:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sharpe Ratio}: $\displaystyle \text{SR} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$ — excess return per unit of risk.
    \item \textbf{Sortino Ratio}: Same as Sharpe but uses downside deviation only.
    \item \textbf{Maximum Drawdown}: Largest peak-to-trough decline in the equity curve.
    \item \textbf{Value-at-Risk (VaR, 95\%)}: The loss threshold exceeded only 5\% of the time.
    \item \textbf{Conditional VaR (CVaR)}: Expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Data}

We use daily dividend- and split-adjusted closing prices for \textbf{Reliance Industries (RELIANCE.NS)} from January 2020 to December 2025, obtained via the \texttt{yfinance} Python library. Reliance was chosen as a large-cap Indian equity with high liquidity.

\subsection{Option Premium Estimation}

Since this project does not use a directly sourced historical option-price dataset for the full sample, we compute theoretical call premiums at each rebalancing date using the Black-Scholes formula. The volatility input $\sigma$ is estimated as the 30-day rolling annualized historical volatility:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}_t = \text{std}\!\left(\ln\frac{S_i}{S_{i-1}}\right)_{i=t-29}^{t} \times \sqrt{252}
\end{equation}
Before the rolling 30-day window is fully available at the start of the sample, we use an expanding realized-volatility estimate based only on returns observed up to that date, plus a fixed initial fallback on the first observation(s). This avoids using future information to initialize the backtest. We assume a constant annual risk-free rate of $r = 7\%$ throughout the analysis. \textbf{This is an important assumption}: actual market premiums may differ from the BS theoretical value due to supply-demand dynamics, changing interest rates, and the volatility smile.

\subsection{Backtesting Procedure}

We implement a \textbf{rolling covered call} strategy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item On day 0, buy the stock at price $S_0$.
    \item Simultaneously sell a call with strike $K = S_0 \times (1 + \delta)$ expiring in 21 trading days, where $\delta$ is the strike offset (e.g., $+5\%$ for OTM). This is our approximation to a one-month roll.
    \item At each expiry:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If $S_T > K$: the call is exercised. The stock is ``called away'' at $K$. Re-purchase at market price and sell a new call.
        \item If $S_T \leq K$: the call expires worthless. Keep the stock and sell a new call.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Track the portfolio value daily and compare with a buy-and-hold benchmark.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Monte Carlo Simulation}

We simulate 10{,}000 stock price paths over the option period using Geometric Brownian Motion:
\begin{equation}
    S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \cdot \exp\!\left[\left(\mu - \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)\Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z\right], \quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the estimated annualized drift from historical data, $\sigma$ is the historical volatility, and $\Delta t = 1/252$. For each path, we compute the covered call return and compare with the stock-only return to build a distribution of outcomes.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

We systematically vary:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Strike offset} from $-10\%$ (deep ITM) to $+15\%$ (far OTM).
    \item \textbf{Days to expiry} from 7 to 90 days.
\end{itemize}
For each combination, we run Monte Carlo simulations and compute an annualized Sharpe ratio to construct a moneyness-expiry heatmap.

\section{Discussion and Interpretation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{When Does the Covered Call Outperform?}

The covered call tends to outperform buy-and-hold in:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flat or sideways markets}: The stock doesn't move much, and the premium income adds to returns that would otherwise be near zero.
    \item \textbf{High-volatility environments}: Higher volatility leads to higher option premiums, so the income from selling calls is greater.
    \item \textbf{Mildly declining markets}: The premium cushion reduces losses compared to holding the stock outright.
\end{itemize}

To move beyond these qualitative claims, we extend the single-ticker analysis with a cross-sectional study across 16 large-cap NSE stocks spanning 7 sectors (Energy, IT, Banking, FMCG, Pharma, Auto, Metals). For each stock we run the same rolling covered-call backtest and define the strategy's return spread as
\[
\alpha_{CC} = R_{CC} - R_{\text{stock-only}}
\]
the difference between the covered-call total return and the buy-and-hold total return over the same window. We use the symbol $\alpha_{CC}$ for brevity, but note that this is a raw return spread and not a risk-adjusted alpha in the CAPM sense.

\subsubsection{Beta and the Covered Call Alpha}

Each stock's beta with respect to the Nifty 50 is estimated by an OLS regression of daily log returns. Figure~\ref{fig:beta_vs_alpha} plots $\alpha_{CC}$ against beta. The slope of the OLS line tells us whether covered-call performance has a systematic relationship with market sensitivity. The sign of this relationship is not obvious a priori: high-beta stocks surrender more upside when rallies break through the strike, but they also command higher implied volatility and therefore larger premiums on each roll. Which effect dominates is an empirical question, and the universe here is deliberately narrow (16 large-cap constituents with betas clustered in a relatively tight range), so the fitted slope should be read as indicative rather than definitive.

\subsubsection{Black-Scholes Pricing}

Because we cannot obtain historical NSE option-chain data through public APIs, the backtest prices each call leg with Black-Scholes using realised 30-day volatility. This makes $\alpha_{CC}$ explicitly model-dependent: the stock-only benchmark does \emph{not} use the option-pricing model, so any systematic mispricing of call premiums feeds directly into the covered-call leg rather than cancelling out. The codebase includes a live snapshot comparison against current listed option prices when the data provider exposes an option chain, but \texttt{yfinance} does not currently return live NSE single-stock option chains for our default ticker, so this diagnostic remains unavailable in practice.

\subsection{When Does It Underperform?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Strong bull markets}: The stock price surges past $K$, and the covered call participates in none of the upside beyond $K$. The opportunity cost can be significant.
    \item \textbf{Severe crashes}: While the premium provides a small cushion, the long stock position still bears the full brunt of a major decline. The downside protection is limited.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Suitable Trader Profiles}

The covered call is suitable for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Income-seeking investors} who want to generate cash flow from their stock holdings.
    \item \textbf{Mildly bullish or neutral investors} who believe the stock will not rally significantly.
    \item \textbf{Portfolio managers} aiming to reduce the volatility of their equity positions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Comparison with the Protective Put}

While the covered call \emph{caps the upside} and provides income, the protective put (long stock + long put) \emph{floors the downside} at a cost. They address opposite concerns:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
     & \textbf{Covered Call} & \textbf{Protective Put} \\
    \midrule
    Upside & Capped at $K$ & Unlimited \\
    Downside & Full exposure (small cushion) & Floored at $K$ \\
    Net premium & Receives $C_0$ & Pays $P_0$ \\
    Market view & Neutral to mildly bullish & Bullish but hedging \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{BS-estimated premiums}: Actual market option premiums differ from theoretical values due to supply-demand effects and the volatility smile.
    \item \textbf{No transaction costs}: We do not model brokerage, bid-ask spreads, or slippage, which would erode returns especially with frequent rolling.
    \item \textbf{European option assumption}: The analysis models European-style calls, which matches the contract style used for NSE stock options; however, the BS model still assumes constant volatility and log-normal returns.
    \item \textbf{Dividend treatment}: The stock-price history is dividend-adjusted, but we do not include an explicit dividend yield in Black-Scholes pricing or separate dividend cash flows in the strategy.
    \item \textbf{Cross-sectional universe}: The multi-ticker analysis is limited to liquid Nifty large-caps with listed options. Results may not generalise to small-caps or stocks without an active options market.
    \item \textbf{No market-price validation of BS premiums}: \texttt{yfinance} does not currently serve live NSE single-stock option chains for our default ticker, so we could not benchmark our theoretical call prices against listed market prices. The BS premiums used in the backtest are therefore unvalidated in absolute terms, and the covered-call leg remains model-dependent.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The covered call strategy systematically exchanges upside potential for immediate premium income and reduced portfolio volatility. Our analysis using historical backtesting, Monte Carlo simulation, and sensitivity analysis on Reliance Industries confirms that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The strategy lowers annualized volatility from 28.27\% to 24.83\% and improves maximum drawdown from -44.08\% to -40.56\% versus buy-and-hold.
    \item The price of that risk reduction is lower annualized return: 11.54\% for the covered call versus 15.39\% for the stock-only benchmark.
    \item The covered call is not a ``free lunch'' — it underperforms in strong rallies and provides only limited protection in crashes.
    \item It is best suited for mildly bullish or neutral investors seeking income generation.
\end{itemize}

The accompanying Streamlit application allows users to explore the strategy with custom parameters and real-time data.
